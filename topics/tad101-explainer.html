The Basics
What Is a Tax Allocation District?

A Tax Allocation District (TAD) is a geographic area where the City of Atlanta reinvests a portion of property tax revenue to fund improvements in that same area. When a TAD is created, the existing property tax base is "frozen." As property values grow over time, the additional tax revenue generated by that growth, called the tax increment, is captured and reinvested back into the district.

TADs do not raise taxes. They redirect a share of the growth in tax revenue that would otherwise flow to the general fund, and put it to work in neighborhoods that need investment most.

Three taxing jurisdictions participate in Atlanta's TADs: the City of Atlanta, Atlanta Public Schools (APS), and Fulton County. APS contributes the largest share, roughly 54% of the increment in most TADs.

TADs are authorized under Georgia's Redevelopment Powers Law and are administered by Invest Atlanta, the City's economic development authority. Each TAD has a defined boundary, a start date, and an estimated closure date when the district is wound down and all tax revenue flows back to the general fund.

Step by Step
How a TAD Works
1
A TAD boundary is established
The City designates a geographic area in need of investment. The existing assessed property value within that boundary becomes the "base value."
2
Property values grow
As new development occurs and existing properties increase in value, the tax base grows above the frozen base value.
3
The increment is captured
The additional tax revenue generated by that growth, the "increment," is collected and held in a dedicated TAD fund rather than flowing to the general fund.
4
Funds are reinvested in the district
TAD funds are used to finance affordable housing, infrastructure, small business support, greenspace, health facilities, and more within the TAD boundary. Funds can be deployed as grants, loans, or bonds.
5
The TAD closes and revenue is returned
When the TAD reaches its end date, all tax revenue from the area flows back to the City, APS, and Fulton County at the full grown value, not the frozen base.

TADs do not raise taxes. They capture the growth in tax revenue that results from investment and reinvest it in the same neighborhoods where that growth is happening. When a TAD closes, the full tax base, including all growth, flows back to the City, APS, and Fulton County.
